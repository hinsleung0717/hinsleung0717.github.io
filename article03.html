意義 Meaningness(1)

lkhmemories — Dec 16, 2025

What's the meaning of life? (This is not an essay arguing for a definitive answer; it simply shares non-personal perspectives from different angles.)

In the documentary "Examined Life", Cornel West responds to this question:

"I think the problem of meaning is very important. Nihilism is a serious challenge. Meaninglessness is a serious challenge. Even making sense of meaninglessness itself is a kind of discipline and achievement. The problem, of course, is that you never reach it. It's not a static, stationary telos — not an end or final aim. It's a process that one never reaches. It's Sisyphean. You're pushing the rock up the hill looking for better meanings or grander, more ennobling, more enabling meanings, but you never reach it. In that sense you die without having the whole, in the language of romantic discourse."

Sisyphus

Such a description leads almost directly to the philosophy of the absurd articulated by Albert Camus in The Myth of Sisyphus. What Camus calls the “absurd” arises precisely from the conflict between humanity’s intense longing for meaning and the universe’s persistent silence in response. The world does not answer, yet human beings continue to ask; this irreconcilable confrontation constitutes the core condition of modern existence. Camus emphasizes that, in the face of the absurd, one must neither turn to religion, metaphysics, or any form of transcendent consolation, nor resort to suicide as a means of escape. The only honest response is to endure the absurd with full lucidity and to persist in revolt.

IN THE FINAL CHAPTER of the book, Camus turns to the figure of Sisyphus from Greek mythology as a symbol of the human condition. Condemned to push a boulder up a mountain only to watch it roll back down again, Sisyphus appears to be engaged in a meaningless task. Yet Camus arrives at a paradoxical conclusion: “The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.” This happiness does not arise from any final outcome, but from the conscious acceptance and affirmation of an absurd fate.

Camus further extends the figure of Sisyphus into modern society: people who repeat the same forms of labor in factories, offices, schools, and examination halls share an equally absurd fate. This condition of life is not tragic in itself; the true tragedy emerges only when one becomes aware of the absurd and yet chooses to deny or flee from it. Like the daily rhythms of living, the absurd is unavoidable. The question, therefore, is not how to eliminate it, but how to learn to live alongside it.

Action persists because human beings continually attempt to compensate for this essential lack. At the core of existence lies a structural deficiency that cannot be resolved. Meaning, then, is not something to be discovered once and for all; it is something to be sought continuously, sustaining the movement of life itself.
