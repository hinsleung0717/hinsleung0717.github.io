藍調時刻 Blue Hour

lkhmemories — Dec 16, 2025

When I travel, I often find that the most memorable moments are not the landmarks themselves, but the quiet intervals in between. One of those moments is what photographers call the blue hour.

The blue hour — a term that comes from the French l’heure bleue — refers to the short period just before sunrise or right after sunset. During this time, the sky takes on a deep, soft blue, and the world seems to slow down. The light is dim but gentle, wrapping buildings and streets in a calm, almost cinematic atmosphere.

What makes the blue hour especially fascinating is that it is not just poetic, but scientific. As sunlight passes through the Earth’s atmosphere at a low angle, certain wavelengths of light — roughly between 575 and 603 nanometers — are absorbed by the ozone layer. What remains is the scattering of shorter blue wavelengths, which dominate the sky and give the scene its distinctive tone.

Yet, science alone cannot explain why this moment feels so special when you are standing there, camera in hand, or simply watching the city change its mood. During the blue hour, streets grow quieter, lights begin to glow, and familiar places feel temporarily unfamiliar. It is a brief pause between day and night, movement and stillness.
